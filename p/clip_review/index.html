Training Method

  • Training

    • Given a batch of N (image, text) pairs, representations of both the images and texts are generated.
    • Instead of using labels, text is used, so there is no need for dataset labeling during dataset creation. Source: Radford, Alec, et al. “Learning transferable visual models from natural language supervision.” International conference on machine learning. PMLR, 2021, Figure 1, (1).
      • An image encoder and a text encoder are trained.
      • The model is trained to maximize the cosine similarity between image embedding and text embedding of the actual N pairs.
      • For each embedding, the output from the [CLS] token of the image encoder is used for the image, and the [EOS] token output from the text encoder is used for text.
      • Contrastive objective: The model is also trained to minimize the cosine similarity between $N^2 - N$ negative image-text pairs.
      • The final symmetric cross entropy loss is obtained by performing both: (1) classifying text embeddings based on image embeddings, and (2) classifying image embeddings based on text embeddings, then calculating cross entropy for both.
    • The larger the mini-batch size, the more detailed the representations become.

  • Inference

    • Obtain the image feature embedding.
    • Obtain the embeddings for all possible texts.
    • Compute the cosine similarity between image and text embeddings.
      Source: Radford, Alec, et al. “Learning transferable visual models from natural language supervision.” International conference on machine learning. PMLR, 2021, Figure 1, (2), (3).
      • Image encoder: vision backbone, generates the feature representation of an image.
      • Text encoder: acts as a linear classifier.
      • Once text encoders are computed, the zero-shot classifier can be cached to reduce inference cost.
    • Inference can be performed without any task-specific training.

Model

  • Image encoder

    • Uses ResNet-50 or Vision Transformer.
    • Output size: 512

  • Text encoder

    • 64M parameters, 12 layers, 512-wide, 8 attention heads
    • Output size: 512
    • Vocabulary size: 49,152
    • Max sequence length: 76

  • Dataset: WebImageText (WIT)

    • 400M image-text pairs collected from the internet.
    • Text must include at least one of 500,000 queries (words that appeared at least 100 times in English Wikipedia).
    • Up to 20,000 (image, text) pairs per query.
    • The word count is similar to that of WebText used in GPT-2 training.

  • Loss: InfoNCE Loss

    • $ {\mathcal{L}}\_{\text{CLIP}}= \frac{1}{2} [ \underbrace{-\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \log \frac{\exp(sim(I_i,T_i)/\tau)} {\sum_{j=1}^{N}\exp(sim(I_i,T_j) / \tau)}}_{\text{image-to-text loss}} + \underbrace{- \frac{1}{N} \sum^{N}\_{i=1} \log \frac{\exp(sim(T_i, I_i)/\tau)}{\sum^{N}\_{j=1} \exp(sim(T_j, I_i) / \tau)}}\_\text{{text-to-image loss}} ] $

  • Parameters

    • Image and text encoders are trained from scratch, without any pre-trained weights.
    • Variants: ResNet-50, ResNet-101, ResNet-50x4, ResNet50x16, ResNet50x64
    • Variants: ViT-B/32, ViT-B/16, ViT-L/14
    • 32 epochs
    • Initial hyperparameters: performed grid search, random search, and manual tuning using 1 epoch of training on the smallest model
    • Mini-batch size: 32,768
      • Since training is performed on alt-text collected from the web, there may be similar images or alt-texts, so large batch training is necessary.

  • Training time

    • CLIP with ResNet50x64 backbone: trained for 18 days on 592 V100 GPUs.
    • CLIP with ViT-L/14 backbone: trained for 12 days on 256 V100 GPUs.

Additional Training Techniques

  • Prompt Engineering
    • In the absence of prompts, homonyms such as “crane” (the bird or the machine) or “major” (department or meaning rank) caused problems.
    • Using prompts like “A photo of a {label}” increased ImageNet accuracy by 1.3%.
    • For datasets with specific categories:
      • Oxford-IIIT: “A photo of a {label}, a type of pet.”
      • Food101: “A photo of a {label}, a type of food.”
      • FGVC Aircraft: “A photo of a {label}, a type of aircraft.”
      • Satellite image classification datasets: “A satellite photo of a {label}.”
  • Ensembling
    • Ensembled several zero-shot classifiers.
      • Examples: “A photo of a big {label}”, “A photo of a small {label}”
      • Ensembled in the embedding space. Used cache to reduce cost.
      • For ImageNet: Used 80 different context prompts and ensembled their predictions, achieving a 3.5% accuracy improvement.

Results

  • Zero-shot performance

    • Compared with a ResNet-50 pretrained on ImageNet and then fine-tuned via linear probing.

      • On many datasets, zero-shot CLIP outperformed.

    • Zero-shot CLIP vs Few-shot linear probe CLIP (average score across 20 datasets).

      • 4-shot CLIP showed performance comparable to zero-shot CLIP.

  • Linear probe CLIP vs state-of-the-art models.

  • Natural Distribution Shift

    • Zero-shot CLIP achieved performance comparable to models trained specifically on ImageNet.
